# Roadmap

A roadmap is an argument about the future, whether people admit it or not.

That is why format matters more than people think. The problem is usually not the colors, the timeline, or the swimlanes. It is that the artifact is doing the wrong job for the wrong audience with the wrong level of certainty. A team roadmap gets shown to executives. A status artifact gets mistaken for strategy. An exploratory direction gets rendered as if it were a commitment.

That is the job of this skill.

`roadmap` helps you choose the right roadmap shape before you start drafting one. It takes the audience, use case, certainty level, horizon, and track structure seriously, and then recommends a roadmap variation that fits the communication job instead of forcing every situation into the same familiar format.

## What it takes seriously

Every roadmap implies something, even when the author did not mean to imply it.

It can imply:

- commitment
- confidence
- sequencing certainty
- organizational alignment
- strategic coherence

This skill helps choose a format that implies the right things and avoids implying the wrong ones.

## How it works

1. **Define the render brief**: identify the audience, use case, horizon, certainty level, and the decision or alignment job the roadmap needs to serve.
2. **Check readiness**: confirm the communication job is clear and the available source material can support the shape being proposed.
3. **Recommend a primary format**: choose the roadmap family that best matches the situation.
4. **Shape the horizon**: decide whether the roadmap should read as now-next-later, quarterly, annual, strategic, or something in between.
5. **Shape the track structure**: decide whether the roadmap should show one dominant thread, multiple strategic tracks, or a portfolio of bets.
6. **Offer variations**: recommend one primary render and one or two credible alternatives, with the tradeoffs made explicit.

The point is not to generate a prettier roadmap. The point is to choose a roadmap that tells the truth about the work and fits the audience that needs to use it.

## Where it earns its keep

- Choosing the right roadmap format for a specific audience or meeting
- Showing different roadmap variations for the same underlying work
- Preventing overcommitment when the situation is still exploratory
- Turning a messy planning brief into a structured roadmap recommendation

## What it should stop

- Treating every roadmap as a timeline
- Presenting uncertain work as committed work
- Confusing a status update with a strategy artifact
- Flattening a multi-track strategy into a misleading single-thread story

## Usage

Describe the audience, what the roadmap needs to help them do, the rough time horizon, and the level of certainty behind the work. Good prompts usually sound like:

> Recommend three roadmap variations for an exec review where we need decision support, not just status.

> Help me choose the right roadmap format for a team alignment discussion.

> Turn this strategy summary into a roadmap recommendation and tell me what the artifact should avoid implying.

The skill will either:

- recommend a primary roadmap render plus alternatives
- outline what each format makes legible and what it hides
- stop and say the communication job is still too unclear for a good roadmap choice
